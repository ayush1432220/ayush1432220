Lucknow : Nawaboo ka Sahar

Lucknow is a city in the northern Indian state of Uttar Pradesh, often referred to as the "City of Nawabs" due to its rich cultural heritage and history.

The term "Nawab" refers to a Muslim ruler or nobleman in India during the Mughal Empire and the British Raj.

Lucknow was one of the major centers of the Mughal Empire, and it flourished under the patronage of the Nawabs of Awadh , who ruled the region for more than a century. The city is famous for its exquisite architecture, especially the monuments and buildings built during the Nawabi era, such as the Bara Imambara, Chhota Imambara, Rumi Darwaza, and the Hussainabad Imambara.

The cuisine of Lucknow is also renowned and is known as Awadhi cuisine, which is characterized by its rich and aromatic flavors. It includes dishes like biryani, kebabs, kormas, and other Mughlai delicacies. Today, Lucknow is a bustling city and the capital of Uttar Pradesh, with a population of over3 million people . It is known for its vibrant culture, traditional arts and crafts, and its role as a center of education, politics, and commerce in Northern India.

The History of lucknow:

The city of Lucknow has a rich and diverse history that dates back to ancient times. According to legend, the city was founded by Lakshmana, the younger brother of Lord Rama, during the period of the Ramayana. The recorded history of Lucknow begins with the arrival of the Mughals in the 16th century. The city was then known as Lakhanpur and was ruled by various local kings and chieftains. It was during the reign of the Mughal Emperor Akbar that the city was renamed as Lucknow and became an important center of trade and commerce. In the 18th century, the region came under the control of the Nawabs of Awadh, who ruled Lucknow for more than a century. The Nawabs were known for their patronage of the arts, literature, and architecture, and under their rule, Lucknow became a cultural hub of Northern India. During the Indian Rebellion of 1857, Lucknow played a significant role, with the city becoming a center of resistance against the British colonial rule. The British eventually regained control of the city, and Lucknow became the capital of the United Provinces of Agra and Oudh. After India gained independence in 1947, Lucknow became the capital of the newly formed state of Uttar Pradesh. Since then, the city has grown and developed into a modern metropolis while retaining its rich cultural heritage and history. Today, Lucknow is a major center of education, commerce, and politics in India.

  1. Imambara

    2. The Imambara in Lucknow, also known as the Bara Imambara, is a historical monument that is considered one of the most significant tourist attractions in the city. It was built in the 18th century by Asaf-ud-Daula, the Nawab of Awadh, and is an excellent example of Mughal architecture. The Imambara consists of a large hall called the Bara Imambara, which is approximately 50 meters long and 15 meters wide. The hall has no pillars and is built entirely of brick and mortar. It is said to be the largest arched hall in the world, with a ceiling that is over 15 meters high. One of the most remarkable features of the Imambara is its intricate labyrinth of corridors, known as the Bhulbhulaiya. Visitors can explore the winding passages and staircases, which were designed to confuse intruders and protect the Nawab 's treasures. Another significant feature of the Imambara is the Asfi Mosque, which is located on the right of the complex. The mosque is known for its beautiful architecture, with intricate carvings and calligraphy adorning its walls. The Imambara is also home to the Rumi Darwaza, a massive gateway that is one of the most iconic symbols of Lucknow. The gateway was built by Asaf-ud-Daula in the 1780s and is modelled on the gateway of the ancient city of Constantinople. Overall, the Imambara in Lucknow is a fascinating example of Mughal architecture and is a must-visit destination for anyone interested in history, culture, and architecture.



  2. Clock Tower

    3. The Clock Tower of Lucknow, also known as the Hussainabad Clock Tower, is a prominent landmark located in the heart of the city. It was built in the 1880s by the British and is a fine example of colonial architecture. The Clock Tower stands at a height of 67 meters and is the tallest clock tower in India. It is made of red sandstone and has an octagonal shape with a cupola on top. The cupola houses a clock that is mounted on a pedestal and is visible from a distance. The clock was made by the London-based clockmaker, Edward John Dent, who also made the famous Big Ben clock in London. The clock has four faces, each measuring 3.5 meters in diameter, and is said to be accurate to within two seconds. The Clock Tower is located in the Hussainabad area of Lucknow, which was once the palace complex of the Nawabs of Awadh. It is surrounded by several other historical monuments, including the Hussainabad Imambara, the Rumi Darwaza, and the Chhota Imambara. The Clock Tower of Lucknow is not only a popular tourist attraction but also a significant landmark that is deeply rooted in the city's history and culture. It is a symbol of the city 's colonial past and serves as a reminder of its rich heritage.


  3. Rumi Gate

    4. The Rumi Gate of Lucknow, also known as the Turkish Gate, is a historic monument located in the old city area of Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh, India. It was built by Nawab Asaf-ud-Daula in 1784, and it is named after the famous 13th-century Muslim poet and scholar Rumi. The Rumi Gate is an ornate gateway that was built to commemorate the arrival of Sir John Shore, the new governor-general of India, in Lucknow. The gate is made of brick and is adorned with decorative embellishments, including intricate carvings, calligraphy, and floral designs. The gate is also known for its imposing height, which is around 60 feet. It has two octagonal towers, each with a domed chhatri or canopy on top, and is surrounded by an arched boundary wall. The Rumi Gate is one of the most iconic symbols of Lucknow, and it is often used as a symbol of the city's rich cultural heritage. It is located near several other historical landmarks, including the Bara Imambara, the Chhota Imambara, and the Clock Tower. Today, the Rumi Gate remains an important landmark in the city of Lucknow and is a popular tourist attraction. It is also used as a venue for cultural events and festivals, such as the Lucknow Mahotsav, which is held annually in the city.


  4. The Residency Lucknow.

    5. The Residency in Lucknow is a historic complex of buildings located in the heart of the city. It was built in the early 19th century as the official residence of the British Resident General of the Court of Awadh during the British colonial period in India. The Residency complex consists of several buildings, including the main residency, an adjacent palace, and several other smaller buildings. The buildings are surrounded by lush gardens and lawns that add to the beauty of the complex. During the Indian Rebellion of 1857, the Residency became a site of fierce fighting between the Indian soldiers and the British forces. The siege of the Residency lasted for 147 days, during which time the British residents and their families, along with many Indian civilians, took refuge in the complex. Today, the Residency is a popular tourist attraction and a symbol of the city's colonial past. The complex has been preserved as a museum, and visitors can explore the buildings and gardens to learn about the history of Lucknow and the events of the Indian Rebellion of 1857. The Residency Museum houses a collection of artifacts, including photographs, documents, and personal belongings of the British residents who lived in the complex. There are also displays of weapons and other items used during the siege of the Residency. The Residency is a must-visit attraction for anyone interested in the history of Lucknow and the colonial period in India.
